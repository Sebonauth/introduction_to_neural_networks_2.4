From Biology to Mathematics

Now that we've seen how biological neurons work, let's do something amazing – let's turn this biological process into mathematics! Don't worry if math isn't your favorite subject; we'll build this bridge step by step.

Think about how you make decisions. When deciding whether to bring an umbrella, you might think: 'Well, the clouds look pretty dark (important factor!), but the forecast says only 20% chance of rain (less important), and I'm only going out for a short time (somewhat important).' You're actually doing something very similar to what our artificial neuron will do – weighing different factors and making a decision.

Let's build our artificial neuron following this same logic. Just like you gave different importance to different factors in the umbrella decision, our artificial neuron assigns 'weights' to its inputs. Here's how it works:

Build Your Vocab

Weights

Numerical values that determine how important each input is to the neuron's decision. Like volume knobs, they can be turned up (more important) or down (less important).

Continue

The mathematical formula looks like this:

\[z = w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + \ldots + w_nx_n + b\]

Don't let this equation scare you! It's just saying: take each input (x), multiply it by how important it is (w), add them all up, and add a little extra number (b) that makes the neuron more or less likely to fire overall.

Stop and Think

Why do you think we need that extra number b (called the bias)? Think about your own decision-making process.

Sometimes we have natural biases in our decisions. You might be generally optimistic or cautious. The bias term b plays a similar role – it makes the neuron naturally more or less likely to fire, regardless of the inputs.
